Camiguin Dive Sites — The 6 Best Diving Spots in Camiguin Island

Six unique dive sites surround Camiguin Island, Philippines — from marine sanctuary reefs teeming with sea turtles to adrenaline-pumping drift dives with pelagics. Dive all of them with Blue Season, your PADI dive center in Camiguin.

Most Popular

Mantigue Island

📍 30 min by boat (~10 km) 🌊 Depth: 5–25m 🏅 All levels 👁️ Visibility: 15–30m

Just 10 km from Blue Season dive center and easily reached within 30 minutes by boat, Mantigue Island is Camiguin's premier snorkeling and diving destination. The island sits inside a protected marine sanctuary, renowned for its clear visibility, healthy coral reefs, and abundant marine life in a calm, scenic setting.

The reef combines vibrant hard and soft coral gardens with sandy slopes and gentle reef edges — visibility often ranges from 15 to 30 meters and currents stay mild to moderate. These relaxed conditions make Mantigue ideal for first-timers, training dives, and experienced divers alike, while the shallow reef also offers excellent snorkeling for non-divers.

More than 20 resident green sea turtles call this sanctuary home, with occasional sightings of impressively large individuals. Alongside the turtles, divers encounter reef fish, moray eels, reef squid, nudibranchs, and schools of pelagic fish cruising the reef edges.

Advanced

Burias Shoal

📍 2 km north of Mantigue 🌊 Depth: 15–40m+ 🏅 Advanced divers 💨 Moderate to strong current

Located 2 kilometers north of Mantigue Island, Burias Shoal is widely regarded as one of the top dive sites around Camiguin. Known for its dramatic topography, moderate to strong currents, and pelagic encounters, it offers a thrilling mix of reef, slope, and open-water action.

The shoal rises from deeper water like a seamount, with sloping reefs that drop into steep walls. Shallower sections hold healthy hard corals, while soft corals and sea fans line the deeper slopes — this structure channels nutrient-rich water and draws both reef life and passing pelagics in abundance.

Burias is where the big encounters happen — schools of jacks and barracuda, trevallies and tuna cruising in blue water, and occasional thresher sharks or reef sharks depending on conditions. Turtles and large reef fish round out what is consistently one of Camiguin's most exciting dives.

Unique

Jigdup Shoal

📍 Northeastern Camiguin 🌊 Depth: 5–30m+ 🏅 All levels 🪸 Slope & Wall

Located on the northeastern side of Camiguin, Jigdup covers two dive sites — Jigdup Slope and Jigdup Wall — making it one of the island's most vibrant and biologically rich destinations. It stands out for its excellent coral health, schooling fish, and classic tropical reef scenery that suits divers of all levels.

The dive typically begins on a gentle coral slope at 5–15 meters, then gradually transitions into a deeper wall. Shallower sections feature extensive hard coral formations — table corals and brain corals — while the deeper wall is draped in colorful soft corals and sea fans, offering both shallow reef exploration and rewarding deeper dives in a single site.

Jigdup is known for higher biodiversity than most Camiguin sites. Schools of fusiliers and jacks patrol the reef edges, while butterflyfish, angelfish, parrotfish, and triggerfish weave through the corals. Look carefully among the sponges for nudibranchs and frogfish, and keep an eye on the blue for the occasional whitetip or tiger shark passing by.

Scenic

White Island

📍 40 min by boat 🌊 Depth: 6–25m 🏅 All levels 👁️ Visibility: 15–25m

White Island is more than a striking white sandbar — it's a rewarding dive site famous for its reef slopes and rich marine biodiversity. Reached in about 40 minutes by boat from our Camiguin dive center, the scenic journey across calm, clear waters ends at one of the island's most visually striking underwater locations.

The gently sloping reef ranges from 6 to 25 meters, making White Island suitable for divers of all levels. Conditions are typically mild and comfortable, with light to moderate currents that occasionally set up the perfect conditions for an easy, enjoyable drift dive.

Beneath the surface the reef reveals vibrant coral formations and distinctive black coral gardens. Schools of butterflyfish are a common sight, while jacks, snappers, groupers, and fusiliers cruise the reef edges. Hawksbill turtles are frequently encountered, and sea snakes weave gracefully through the corals. Divers who look closer will find macro treasures — ribbon and moray eels, mantis shrimp, scorpionfish, and delicate cowry shells tucked among the reef.

White Island remains a highly sought-after dive on our schedule, prized for its balance of accessibility, marine diversity, and the chance of unexpected encounters. On rare occasions, lucky divers have even reported manta rays and whale sharks passing through these waters.

Macro Paradise

Cabuan / Giant Clam

📍 Southeast coast of Camiguin 🌊 Depth: 3–18m 🏅 All levels 📷 Macro diving

Located off the southeast coast of Camiguin, Cabuan Reef is best known as a giant clam sanctuary — one of the most ecologically distinctive dive sites on the island. The shallow, accessible reef makes it a relaxed and rewarding dive for all levels.

The star attraction is Cabuan's population of giant Tridacna clams — the largest living bivalves in the world, growing to over a meter in length. Their iridescent mantles display striking patterns of blue, green, and brown under sunlight. These clams are protected under CITES, and Cabuan serves as an active conservation zone supporting their recovery.

Beyond the clams, the reef rewards patient divers with a rich variety of macro life. Nudibranchs, frogfish, and scorpionfish hide among the rubble and coral heads, schools of snapper drift overhead, and moray eels peer from crevices. Sea snakes are also a common and graceful sight weaving through the reef.

Historic

Sunken Cemetery

📍 Western coast of Camiguin 🌊 Depth: 5–20m 🏅 All levels 🌋 1871 Volcanic origin

Located off the western coast of Camiguin, the Sunken Cemetery is one of the Philippines' most atmospheric and historically significant dive sites. In 1871, an eruption of Mount Vulcan caused the coastline to subside, submerging the old town of Catarman — and its cemetery — beneath the sea. A large cross rising above the water marks the site today.

Underwater, scattered remnants of gravestones and stone structures lie partially covered by coral and marine growth. Over time these submerged ruins have acted as an artificial reef, with patches of soft corals, sponges, and hard corals forming small ecosystems around the old cemetery walls. The site has a calm, almost surreal quality — more contemplative than dramatic, and unlike any other dive in the region.

Schools of batfish circle the submerged cross, sea turtles rest on the reef, and pufferfish drift lazily among the ruins. Look closer and you'll find lionfish perched on ledges, scorpionfish camouflaged on the rubble, moray eels coiled in crevices, and nudibranchs dotting the encrusted stones.
